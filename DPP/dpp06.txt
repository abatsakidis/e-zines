    MILWAUKEE- There are some people the criminal justice system simply
cannot straighten out. Gary A. Medrow is one of them.
    For more than 30 years, Medrow has been in and out of court, jail
and prison.
    He always comes back.
    "I do want help very much," Medrow told Circuit Judge Dominic S.
Amato last week during his latest courtroom visit. "I want to make
something out of my life. I want to stop hurting people."
    But the skinny, 54-year-old man with long, stringy hair doesn't hurt
his crime victims in the classic sense. In fact, there is nothing
classic about what he does.
    Medrow's crime for the past 30 years has been picking up telephones,
calling women and trying to persuade them to lift other women and carry
them around. He makes the calls while posing as a police officer,
traffic accident investigator, or motor vehicle licensing official, and
he's done it throughout the state. He's been arrested for the calls
dozens of times since 1968. No one has ever figured out for certain why
he gets a certain sexual thrill from the incedents.
    "Gary has become quite familliar with various local jails and state
prison facilities, at times under conditions that are fairly liberal,
and on other occasions experiencing total isolation, which he
acknowledges has been rather difficult for him to cope with,"
psychologist Itzhak Matusiak wrote in a recent report to Amato.
    Medrow's latest trip through the criminal justice system shows how
perplexing he has been to the system. He was charged in January 1997
with impersonating a police officer, accused of telephoning a coffee
shop employee at St. Luke's Medical Center, posing as a cop and trying
to talk her into having another woman lift her and carry her around.
    While the case was still pending, Medrow picked up 24 new charges
for a series of phone calls last September with the same purpose.
    With those cases pending before Amato in March, Medrow traveled to
Tomah, Wis., to undergo inpatient treatment at the U.S. Department of
Veterans Affairs hospital there, according to his attorney, Michael F.
Hart. But while staying in a Tomah motel, awaiting treatment, Medrow
made a series of telephone calls to women in three states, according to
court records.
    Medrow was arrested and charged with six new criminal counts in
Monroe County Circuit Court. When Medrow got back to Milwaukee, Amato
raised his bail to $240,000, leaving Medrow locked up for the next 42
days.
    Last week, after Hart had worked out an agreement with prosecutors
to have his client undergo inpatient treatment, Medrow pleaded not
guilty by reason of mental disease to charges of impersonating an
officer and unlawful use of a telephone. Amato then committed Medrow to
a state mental hospital for treatment.
    "Prison does him no good," Hart said. "I think that's everyone's
analysis. He gets nothing in the prison system for his type of problem."
    Hart and prosecutors hope that as Medrow spends several months in a
mental hospital, personnel will get a handle on his problem, treat it
and determine the proper type of medication.
